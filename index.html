From Poptropica to Duke: A Computer Science Journey

Getting Started

Looking back on it, I couldn't tell you when I first began using a computer. I can remember using them almost as far back as my memories go, but I know I didn't grow up totally surrounded by technology like kids these days do. If I had to put a specific time on it, I would say I first began using a computer at the start of elementary school, mainly to play games like Poptropica or whatever was popular on Miniclip. My computer interests matured throughout the few years after that, and it was in fourth grade when I first became interested in computer science (albeit a very rudimentary version). I got involved with Lego Robotics and quickly fell in love with the programming aspect. For a shy kid with glasses, there was something very empowering about being able to make a program that would precisely control a little robot. I continued with Lego Robotics through middle school (and in high school as an assistant coach) and took my first true computer science course as a freshman in high school. The rest (and to be fair, everything I already mentioned) is history.

Computing Experience

Everything seems to be a computer these days, so I'll just give you the highlights. I've used Windows computers, Macs, and some Linux (I thought it would be cool to dual boot my laptop with Ubuntu for a while). I have an iPhone (that I use more than I would like to), as well as an iPad. I plan on changing that.

I'm hoping this class will force that jump and that, at the end of it, I will have the knowledge to finally make use of the domain name I bought months ago (www.jonathanrbrowning.com). I've become deeply interested in design (including user experience design, open design, and human-centered design) recently, as well as the education space at large; I suppose the ultimate goal would be figuring out how to combine my newer interests with software development.

(My guess about) The Life of a Web Developer

I would assume that web developers work with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript on a regular basis. But I would also assume that they don't just spend all day typing away, making minor tweaks to a website. Websites, unlike some other code, is inherently human-facing, so there should be a strong human element in the web developer's day. Whether that's the boss talking about new features they want, or a small-business client describing how they want their website to look depends on where the developer works. Outside of the aforementioned HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, I don't really have a good idea of what's needed to code different parts of a website. If I was a betting man, I would bet that SQL is used in some respect for some of the databases that are behind the scenes of websites.

Looking for inspiration (on building websites)

  • Websites I like
    • The Duke Athletics homepage is one of several websites I frequent as a sportswriter for The Chronicle. The colors are sufficiently contrasting and it's easy to nagivate. The most recent news is front and center, and there's an easy to use taskbar at the top whenever I want to look more depth at a certain sport.
    • Much like their products, Apple's website is visually pleasing and is intuitive to use. There's a focus on images on the homepage, with enough links to other parts of the site to get you know where you need to go. There are splashes of color, but nothing distracting to the eyes. While it would be a disservice to call Apple a "user experience company" when they're revolutionzing consumer-grade ARM chips, a good deal of what makes them so appealing is the experience that comes from using their products, not just the end result you get.
  • Websites that leave something to be desired
    • The Kentucky DMV site (along with many other government sites) is one of my least favorites that I unfortunately had to frequent recently when renewing my license. It's not the ugliest site ever, but there's a number of different colors that don't entirely go together, there's a plethora of links all over the main page, multiple search boxes on the page, and overall, it's just confusing to navigate. Nothing about it screams "modern."
    • For a website that I've been hearing my dad talk about since I was a kid, Craiglist's website is in sore need of an update. The colors aren't bad, but it's not intuitive to use at all. The homepage is inundated with links to way too many different options. It looks like something that someone who just learned HTML would put together, not a company that's been around longer than I've been alive.

Tutorials

  • One of the main issues of the digital age is the sheer number of logins that each individual has. To keep track of them all and all the associated passwords, there have been two main options. 1) Use a password manager such as LastPass or 2) use the same password/variation of the same password for every site. The first option is a great one, but is difficult to get started with. However, SSO (Single Sign On), has made this much easier, allowing one account (Duke account, Google account, AppleID, etc) to log you into a variety of different sites. I think implementing a feature like this into our websites could be super interesting - this tutorial provides a starting point for doing that.
  • Another common issue of the digital age is an increasing risk of online security breaches. Other than having a strong password (and using a password manager), another good idea is two enable 2FA (two-factor authentication). If our websites are to handle any sensitive data, I would consider 2FA an essential piece in implementing the site. This tutorial may be able to help with that.
